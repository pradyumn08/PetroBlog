Understanding Vertically Fractured Wells with Rectangle SRV: A First Principles Approach

In this blog post, we'll break down a complex concept from petroleum engineering: the pressure transient analysis of vertically fractured wells (VFW) with rectangle stimulated reservoir volume (SRV). We'll explain how these wells behave in tight oil reservoirs, starting from first principles and building up to a comprehensive understanding that even beginners can grasp.

The Problem We're Solving

Imagine you have an oil reservoir deep underground, but the rock is so tight (low permeability) that oil barely flows through it. This is what engineers call a "tight oil reservoir." To extract oil efficiently, engineers create fractures in the rock to increase the flow paths. The region where these fractures are created is called the Stimulated Reservoir Volume (SRV).

But here's the challenge: How do we model and predict the pressure behavior in these wells over time? Understanding this is crucial for:

  • Estimating how much oil we can recover
  • Optimizing fracturing operations
  • Planning well spacing and development strategies

Fig. 1: Conceptual diagram of a vertically fractured well with rectangle SRV

Traditional Approaches vs. The New Model

Most existing models assume that the SRV is circular, but real-world micro-seismic monitoring shows that the SRV is actually more rectangular. This mismatch can lead to inaccurate predictions.

The paper we're exploring proposes a new analytical model that:

  1. Models the SRV as a rectangle (more accurate to reality)
  2. Accounts for a bi-wing fracture (the main fracture created during stimulation)
  3. Considers stress-sensitivity of the rock (how permeability changes with pressure)
  4. Models the unsteady flow between the rock matrix and fractures

Building the Model from First Principles

1. The Physical Model

Let's start by understanding what we're modeling physically:

Fig. 2: Five-region model for a vertically fractured well with rectangle SRV

The model divides the reservoir into two main regions:

  1. Inner Region (SRV): This is the stimulated part with higher permeability. It's modeled as a dual-porosity system, meaning we consider both the rock matrix and the fracture network separately.
  2. Outer Region: This is the unstimulated part with very low permeability. It's modeled as a single-porosity system.

A vertical bi-wing fracture runs through the center of the SRV, connecting to the wellbore. This is the main conduit for oil flow.

2. The Five-Linear-Flow Model

To simplify the complex flow patterns, the model divides the system into five regions with linear flow:

  • Hydraulic Fracture: The main bi-wing fracture
  • Inner Region f: The fractured region parallel to the hydraulic fracture
  • Outer Region 1: Unstimulated region beyond the inner region, parallel to the hydraulic fracture
  • Outer Region 2: Unstimulated region perpendicular to the hydraulic fracture
  • Outer Region 3: Unstimulated region diagonal to the hydraulic fracture

3. Key Mathematical Concepts

Before diving into the equations, let's understand some key concepts:

Permeability Stress-Sensitivity

As pressure decreases during production, the effective stress on the rock increases, which can reduce permeability. This is modeled using an exponential relationship:

\[ k = k_i e^{-\alpha(p_i - p)} \]

Where:

  • \(k\) is the current permeability
  • \(k_i\) is the initial permeability
  • \(\alpha\) is the stress-sensitivity coefficient
  • \(p_i\) is the initial pressure
  • \(p\) is the current pressure

Dual-Porosity Model

In the SRV region, we consider two interacting continua:

  • The matrix: Contains most of the oil but has low permeability
  • The fracture network: Has high permeability but low storage capacity

Oil flows from the matrix to the fracture network, and then to the wellbore.

The Mathematical Model

Now let's build the mathematical model step by step:

1. Governing Equations for Each Region

For each region, we need to establish:

  • Mass balance equations (continuity equations)
  • Flow equations (Darcy's law)
  • Boundary conditions

Let's start with the outer region (Region 3) as an example:

\[ e^{-\alpha_{D}P_{3D}}\left[{\frac{\partial^{2}P_{_{3D}}}{\partial x_{_{D}}^{2}}}-\alpha_{\mathrm{D}}({\frac{\partial P_{_{3D}}}{\partial x_{_{D}}}})^{2}\right]={\frac{1}{n_{_{m D}}}}{\frac{\partial P_{_{3D}}}{\partial t_{_{D}}}} \]

This equation looks complex, but it's essentially saying:

"The rate of pressure change over time (right side) equals the spatial variation of pressure (left side), adjusted for how permeability changes with pressure (the exponential term)."

The boundary conditions for Region 3 are:

\[ \left.\frac{\partial P_{3D}}{\partial x_{D}}\right|_{x_{D}=x_{O D}}=0 \] \[ \left.P_{3D}\right|_{x_{D}=x_{I D}}=\left.P_{2D}\right|_{x_{D}=x_{I D}} \]

These conditions mean:

  • No flow across the outer boundary (first equation)
  • Pressure continuity at the interface with Region 2 (second equation)

2. Solving the Non-Linear Equations

The stress-sensitivity term makes these equations non-linear and difficult to solve directly. The paper uses Pedrosa's substitution to linearize them:

\[ P_{D} = -\frac{1}{\alpha_{D}} \ln(1 - \alpha_{D} \xi_{D}) \]

After this substitution and applying Laplace transformation, the equations become more manageable. For Region 3, we get:

\[ \overline{{\xi}}_{_{3D}}=\overline{{\xi}}_{_{2D}}\Big\vert_{x_{D}=x_{I D}}\frac{\cosh[\sqrt{s/n_{m D}}(x_{O D}-x_{D})]}{\cosh[\sqrt{s/n_{m D}}(x_{O D}-x_{I D})]} \]

This solution represents the pressure behavior in Region 3 in the Laplace domain.

3. Connecting All Regions

By solving similar equations for all regions and applying appropriate boundary conditions at the interfaces, we can connect the solutions to get the complete pressure response of the system.

The final bottom hole pressure expression in the Laplace domain is:

\[ \overline{{\xi}}_{\mathrm{w}D}=\frac{\pi}{C_{F D}\cdot s\cdot\sqrt{\gamma_{6}}\tanh[\sqrt{\gamma_{6}}y_{I D}]} \]

Where \(\gamma_6\) is a complex function of various parameters that captures the interactions between all regions.

To get the pressure in the real time domain, we need to perform numerical inversion of the Laplace transform:

\[ P_{w D}(t_{D})=-\frac{1}{\alpha_{D}}\ln[1-\alpha_{D}\xi_{w D}(t_{D})] \]

Flow Regimes: Understanding the Pressure Behavior

One of the most valuable insights from this model is identifying distinct flow regimes that occur during production:

  1. Wellbore storage effect flow: Early-time behavior dominated by wellbore storage
  2. Skin and hydraulic fracture conductivity effect flow: Shows as a "hump" in the derivative curve
  3. SRV width effect flow: Linear flow in the SRV, appearing as a "concave" in the derivative curve
  4. Inner boundary dominated flow: When pressure depletion reaches the boundaries of the SRV
  5. Outer tight oil reservoir effect flow: Flow from the unstimulated region
  6. Outer boundary dominated flow: When pressure depletion reaches the outer boundaries

Key Insights and Practical Applications

What We've Learned

From the model and simulations, we can draw several important conclusions:

  1. Hydraulic Fracture Conductivity: Higher conductivity reduces the "hump" in the pressure derivative curve and weakens the SRV width effect. This means better fracture conductivity improves early-time flow performance.
  2. SRV Width: Larger SRV width extends the duration of the second flow regime and weakens the inner boundary dominated flow. This confirms that creating a larger stimulated volume improves production.
  3. Stress Sensitivity: Higher stress sensitivity accelerates the appearance of outer boundary dominated flow and reduces the duration of outer reservoir effect flow. This highlights the importance of accounting for stress sensitivity in tight reservoirs.
  4. Outer Region Dimensions: The dimensions of the outer unstimulated region affect the timing of boundary-dominated flow, which helps in determining optimal well spacing.

Real-World Applications

This model provides several practical benefits for reservoir engineers:

  • Fracture Design Optimization: By understanding how fracture conductivity and SRV dimensions affect flow behavior, engineers can design better fracturing operations.
  • Well Spacing Determination: The model helps predict when interference between wells might occur, allowing for optimal well spacing.
  • Reservoir Characterization: By matching actual pressure data with the model, engineers can estimate reservoir properties like permeability and stress sensitivity.
  • Production Forecasting: Understanding the different flow regimes allows for more accurate production forecasts.

Example: Analyzing a Real Well

In the paper, the model was applied to a real well from the Ordos Basin in China. By matching the pressure data, they were able to determine:

  • The actual dimensions of the SRV (64m width × 160m length)
  • The fracture conductivity
  • The permeability contrast between the SRV and unstimulated rock

These parameters were slightly different from what was estimated from micro-seismic monitoring, highlighting the value of pressure transient analysis for validation.

Conclusion: Why This Matters

The rectangle SRV model with bi-wing fracture provides a more accurate representation of vertically fractured wells in tight oil reservoirs compared to traditional circular models. By accounting for stress sensitivity and unsteady cross-flow, it captures the complex physics of these systems while remaining computationally efficient.

For reservoir engineers, this model offers a powerful tool for:

  • Interpreting pressure transient tests
  • Evaluating fracturing effectiveness
  • Optimizing well spacing and field development
  • Forecasting production more accurately

As tight oil continues to be an important resource globally, models like this will play a crucial role in maximizing recovery while minimizing development costs.
