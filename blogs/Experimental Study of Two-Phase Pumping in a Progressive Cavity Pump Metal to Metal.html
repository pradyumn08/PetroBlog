The Surprising Physics of Two-Phase Pumping: How Gas Can Actually Improve Pump Performance

You know, I've always been fascinated by how things work. Not just theoretically, but in the real, nuts-and-bolts, oil-and-metal world. Today, I want to talk about something that might seem mundane at first glance but is actually a beautiful example of how physics works in industrial machinery: progressive cavity pumps.

Now, don't tune out just because I said "pumps" - this is fascinating stuff! These pumps have a curious behavior when pumping mixtures of liquid and gas that defies our intuition. Sometimes, adding gas to a liquid actually makes the pump work better. How can that be? That's what we're going to explore today.

What's a Progressive Cavity Pump Anyway?

First things first. A progressive cavity pump (PCP) consists of two main parts: a rotor and a stator. The rotor is like a corkscrew that turns inside the stator, which has a matching helical shape. As the rotor turns eccentrically inside the stator, it creates cavities that move from the suction end to the discharge end, carrying fluid along.

A typical progressive cavity pump showing the helical rotor inside the stator.

The paper we're looking at today focuses on a special type called a "metal-to-metal" PCP. In conventional PCPs, the stator is made of an elastomer (rubber-like material), but in a metal-to-metal PCP, both the rotor and stator are made of metal with a small clearance between them.

This clearance is defined mathematically as:

\[ w = \frac{d_r - d_e}{2} \]

Where \(d_r\) is the rotor diameter and \(d_e\) is the stator diameter. When \(w < 0\), we have what's called an "interference fit" (typical in elastomer PCPs), and when \(w > 0\), we have a clearance (typical in metal-to-metal PCPs).

The Puzzle of Two-Phase Flow

Now, here's where it gets interesting. When pumping a mixture of liquid and gas (called "two-phase flow"), something counterintuitive happens. The researchers found that under certain conditions, adding gas to the liquid actually improved the pump's volumetric efficiency!

Let me explain what volumetric efficiency means. It's simply the ratio of what actually comes out of the pump to what theoretically should come out based on its geometry. No pump is perfect - some fluid always slips backward through the clearances between the rotor and stator. This "slip flow" reduces efficiency.

The gas void fraction (GVF) is the proportion of gas in the total flow:

\[ \text{GVF} = \frac{Q_{\text{GAS}}}{Q_{\text{GAS}} + Q_{\text{LIQ}}} \]

Where \(Q_{\text{GAS}}\) is the gas flow rate and \(Q_{\text{LIQ}}\) is the liquid flow rate.

The Experimental Setup

The researchers built a test rig to measure how the pump performed with different mixtures of oil and air, at different speeds and pressures. They measured the flow rates, pressures, and even installed pressure sensors at various points along the pump to see what was happening inside.

What they found was remarkable. At certain operating conditions (particularly at higher speeds like 300-400 rpm), adding gas (up to about 50% GVF) actually increased the total flow rate compared to pumping just liquid!

This is counterintuitive. You might expect that adding gas would make the pump less efficient because gas is compressible and can "slip" more easily through clearances. But that's not what happened!

The Physics Behind It

To understand this phenomenon, we need to think about what's happening inside the pump. When there's only liquid, some of it slips backward through the clearances between the rotor and stator. This slip flow reduces efficiency.

But when gas is added, something interesting happens. The researchers observed that the pressure profiles inside the pump became smoother, and the pressure spikes at the sealing lines decreased. This suggests that gas is acting as a sort of "cushion" or "shock absorber" for pressure changes.

Here's my explanation: When gas is present, some of the energy that would have been lost to slip flow is instead used to compress the gas. The compressed gas takes up less volume, which means there's less fluid available to slip backward. This improves the volumetric efficiency!

The power consumed by the pump can be broken down into:

\[ P_E = Q_{\text{LIQ}} \cdot \Delta P + \left(\int Q_{\text{GAS}} \cdot dP \right) \]

For an isothermal process (which is a reasonable approximation here since the temperature change was less than 2Â°C), the gas compression power is:

\[ P_{\text{C,ISOT}} = Q_{\text{GAS}} \cdot P_{\text{SUC}} \cdot \ln\left(\frac{P_{\text{DIS}}}{P_{\text{SUC}}}\right) \]

Where \(P_{\text{SUC}}\) is the suction pressure and \(P_{\text{DIS}}\) is the discharge pressure.

The losses due to slip can be calculated as:

\[ P_{\text{S,LIQ}} = S_{\text{LIQ}} \cdot \Delta P \]

Where \(S_{\text{LIQ}}\) is the liquid slip flow rate.

The Limits of the Effect

This beneficial effect doesn't continue indefinitely. As the differential pressure increases, the advantage of having gas in the flow diminishes and eventually disappears. At high differential pressures, the gas slip becomes significant, and the efficiency drops.

Also, when the gas void fraction gets too high (above about 80%), the pump's performance deteriorates significantly. The researchers found it difficult to obtain stable operating points at GVF = 0.8.

Pressure Distribution Along the Pump

Another fascinating aspect of this research was the measurement of pressure along the pump. For both single-phase and two-phase flow, the pressure increased linearly from the suction end to the discharge end, indicating that the slip flow is constant along the pump.

This differs from what has been observed in elastomer stator PCPs and twin-screw pumps, where the pressure distribution with two-phase flow is non-linear. This linear pressure distribution reinforces the idea that in metal-to-metal PCPs, gas slip occurs even at low gas void fractions.

Practical Implications

So what does all this mean in practice? For engineers working with progressive cavity pumps in oil production, especially for heavy oil that may contain gas, this research provides valuable insights:

  1. Metal-to-metal PCPs can effectively handle two-phase flow, making them a viable alternative to elastomer stator PCPs in certain applications.
  2. The presence of some gas (up to about 50% GVF) can actually improve the pump's performance under certain conditions.
  3. Operating the pump at higher speeds (300-400 rpm) generally improves volumetric efficiency.
  4. There's an optimal differential pressure for maximum hydraulic power, beyond which efficiency drops rapidly.

The Beauty of Fluid Dynamics

What I find most beautiful about this research is how it reveals the complex interplay between gas compressibility, fluid dynamics, and mechanical design. The counterintuitive result - that adding gas can improve pump performance - reminds us that nature often has surprises in store when we investigate carefully.

This is what science is all about: making careful observations, challenging our assumptions, and revising our understanding based on evidence. The researchers didn't just accept the conventional wisdom that gas would reduce pump efficiency; they tested it experimentally and discovered something unexpected.
